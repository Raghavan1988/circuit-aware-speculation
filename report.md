# Acceptance-Aware Speculation: Results Summary

Prepared 2026-07-13 (supersedes the 2026-07-12 version). Models: Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (target) with a pool of Qwen2.5 drafts (0.5B and 1.5B, general and specialized). Compute: Modal, A100.

## Summary

All three roadmap research questions now have answers, and the work surfaced several findings the roadmap did not anticipate. In brief: (1) adaptive draft length works: a simple, training-free entropy rule beats the best fixed draft length by 11.2 percent efficiency on held-out data and cuts wasted drafting by 62 percent; (2) draft-model routing does not work on this setup: domain-specialized drafts tie or lose to a same-size generalist, even on their own home domain, so the "route the draft" idea has no headroom here; (3) the joint question therefore reduces to the length question; (4) the earlier core findings stand: acceptance has strong, interpretable structure by token type (the atlas), cheap free signals predict acceptance well and internal-state probes do not beat them, and the research harness makes the small draft artificially expensive to run (a launch-bound effect), which defers any wall-clock speed claim. We also built a much more representative evaluation corpus (version 2: 1,494 prompts, 7 workload axes, 9 datasets, license-audited), and a replication of the draft-routing result on that corpus is running now.

## 1. The research questions and where they stand

| RQ | Question | Answer | Key evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| RQ2 | Does adaptive draft length beat the best fixed length? | Yes | +11.2 percent efficiency held-out; 62 percent less wasted drafting; generalizes dev to test (+11.3 to +11.2) |
| RQ3 | Does routing among domain-specialized drafts add more? | No | Specialists tie or lose to a size-matched generalist; the math specialist is significantly worse even on math; an oracle router equals the general 1.5B draft in every domain |
| RQ1 | Does the joint (draft + length) controller beat the best fixed policy in net speedup? | Collapses to RQ2 | The draft axis adds nothing (RQ3), and the net-speedup metric is blocked by the launch-bound harness |

## 2. What we built

- An exact greedy speculative-decoding engine, proven token-identical to running the target alone (73 of 73 equivalence checks in full precision).
- A sealed corpus of 644 prompts across code, math, chat, and summarization, decoded under 8 policies, yielding about 302,000 labelled draft tokens as immutable, checksummed data.
- An offline policy-replay evaluator: because every round records the target's choices at all drafted positions, any length policy can be evaluated exactly, without re-running a model.
- A draft-pool comparison harness: all candidate drafts are scored on the identical target continuation, so comparisons are apples to apples.
- A version-2 corpus (1,494 prompts, 7 axes, 9 public datasets) with per-dataset license metadata, built as a separate versioned artifact.
- A measurement-first discipline: every reported number is generated by a script from sealed data, and negative results are recorded in the claims ledger.

## 3. Main results

### 3.1 Adaptive draft length beats the best fixed length (new, answers RQ2)

We replayed length policies over the sealed traces, tuned the entropy threshold on the development split, froze it, and evaluated on the held-out test split, using efficiency metrics that do not depend on wall-clock timing (with drafting priced at a serving-realistic fraction of verification cost).

| Policy (held-out test) | Tokens per round | Wasted per emitted token | Efficiency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Best fixed length (8) | 4.32 | 1.08 | 2.40 |
| Entropy-stop controller | 3.85 | 0.41 | 2.67 (+11.2 percent) |
| Oracle (per-round best) | 4.32 | 0.02 | 3.23 |

The controller beats the best fixed length by 11.2 percent (11.3 on dev, so it transfers without retuning), cuts wasted draft tokens by about 62 percent while keeping about 89 percent of the yield, and captures about one third of the oracle headroom. It also wins within every individual domain. Recent-acceptance history alone adds only 0.5 percent: the draft's own entropy is the signal that matters.

### 3.2 Specialized drafts do not beat a generalist (new, answers RQ3)

We measured a full draft-by-domain acceptance matrix: five tokenizer-compatible drafts, each scored on the same target continuations, with size-matched statistical comparisons.

| Draft | chat | code | math | summarization |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| general 0.5B | 0.738 | 0.898 | 0.910 | 0.672 |
| Coder 0.5B | 0.675 | 0.899 | 0.877 | 0.603 |
| general 1.5B | 0.789 | 0.917 | 0.928 | 0.736 |
| Math 1.5B | 0.662 | 0.862 | 0.919 | 0.554 |
| Coder 1.5B | 0.753 | 0.917 | 0.917 | 0.695 |

Findings, with prompt-grouped 95 percent confidence intervals: the code specialists statistically tie their size-matched generalist on code (differences of about minus 0.003 to minus 0.004, intervals covering zero); the math specialist is significantly worse than the same-size generalist even on math (minus 0.009, interval entirely below zero); and a perfect per-domain router would simply pick the general 1.5B everywhere, so routing among off-the-shelf specialists adds essentially nothing. The mechanism is instructive: acceptance measures how well the draft matches the target model's own distribution, and a general-instruct draft from the same family already matches a general-instruct target better than a domain expert does. Draft size helps; domain specialization does not. A replication on the larger version-2 corpus (1,494 prompts, including translation, retrieval QA, and structured-output domains) is running now.

### 3.3 The acceptance atlas (headline positive finding)

Acceptance rate varies systematically from 0.52 to 0.88 depending on the kind of token being drafted, measured over 302,464 tokens in 38 category-by-phase cells with confidence intervals. Structural tokens are easy (code delimiters, operators, numbers: about 0.85 to 0.88); semantic and surprising tokens are hard (named entities, reasoning transitions: about 0.58 to 0.67). This is the measured mechanism behind the adaptive-length win, and it is independent of any timing question.

### 3.4 Cheap signals are strong; internal-state probes do not beat them

Free signals the draft already produces (its entropy, confidence margin, and recent acceptance history) predict token acceptance at AUROC about 0.87 on the probe subset. Linear probes on the draft's internal representations peak at 0.803 (layer 18 of 24) and add at most 0.006 when combined with the cheap signals. This is a well-powered null result: for this model pair, the linearly accessible acceptance information is already in the free signals.

### 3.5 The research harness makes the small draft artificially expensive (systems finding)

Running the 0.5B draft one token at a time costs about 24 ms per token, nearly the cost of one 7B verification pass (about 30 ms), because single-token decoding is dominated by fixed per-layer dispatch overhead rather than compute. Fused attention and standard compilation do not fix it; the real fix is a fixed-shape cache plus graph capture, which is deliberately deferred serving engineering. Consequence: adaptive routing is worth only about 5 percent wall-clock on this harness, but the same acceptance data implies 25 to 46 percent at serving-grade draft cost. We therefore report efficiency (Section 3.1) rather than wall-clock speedup, and state this limitation openly.

### 3.6 A representative corpus, version 2 (new)

A systematic survey (127 candidate datasets, 94 verified with licenses) concluded that the original 4-domain corpus under-represents the acceptance extremes that adaptive methods exploit. We built corpus version 2: 1,494 prompts across 7 axes (code, math, chat, translation, retrieval QA, structured JSON output, summarization) from 9 public datasets, each with license and provenance metadata, plus a licenses manifest. Copyright-sensitive sources are marked to ship row identifiers rather than raw text. The original corpus and all results on it remain intact; version 2 is a separate versioned artifact.

## 4. What this means

The contribution is now a coherent story with positive and negative results that reinforce each other:

1. Acceptance has strong, interpretable structure (the atlas), and a training-free controller that reads a cheap signal converts that structure into a real efficiency win over any fixed policy (RQ2, positive).
2. Two natural-sounding upgrades do not pay: internal-state probes do not beat free signals, and domain-specialized drafts do not beat a same-size generalist (RQ3, negative). Both are well-powered nulls that save the community effort and sharpen where the real levers are: draft size, target matching, and length adaptation.
3. The speed story is honestly scoped: efficiency gains are demonstrated now; wall-clock gains await a serving-grade draft path, and we quantify exactly how much is waiting (25 to 46 percent).

## 6. Risks

- Version-2 results could shift version-1 conclusions. Mitigation: identical protocol on both corpora; the routing replication is already running; any differences will be reported, not smoothed over.
- The wall-clock speedup claim remains blocked until the serving-grade draft path exists. We will not state speed numbers from the current harness beyond the launch-bound characterization.
- Several v2 loaders currently stream data without pinned revisions; results runs on v2 require pinning first (recorded requirement).
- The known split-labeling bug in the sweep runner must be fixed before any new sweep; the existing corpus recovers the split at analysis time, so no current result is affected.
- The Llama replication remains blocked: access to the 1B draft model repository is still not granted (the 8B target appears approved). The findings are currently single-family (Qwen).
- Field velocity: adjacent speculative-decoding papers appear monthly; the staged-release policy (preprint when science gates pass) mitigates being scooped on the remaining unpublished pieces.
- Budget: the remaining discretionary spend is the v2 sweep; all other next steps are CPU-level costs.
